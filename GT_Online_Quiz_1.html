1. What is the formal definition of Game Theory?Easy
A) The study of board games and their strategies
B) The mathematical study of strategic interaction between rational decision-makers
C) The analysis of economic markets only
D) The psychology of competitive behavior
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

Game theory is formally defined as the mathematical study of strategic interaction between rational decision-makers (agents). While it applies to many domains including economics, its core focus is on strategic interactions where outcomes depend on the choices of multiple agents.

2. In the TCP Congestion Control Prisoner's Dilemma, what is the Nash Equilibrium?Easy
A) (C, C) - Both use Correct TCP
B) (C, D) - Mixed strategies
C) (D, C) - Mixed strategies
D) (D, D) - Both use Defective TCP
Explanation:

Correct Answer: D

The Nash Equilibrium occurs at (D, D) where both players choose the defective TCP implementation. At this point, neither player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, even though both would be better off at (C, C).

3. Which of the following is NOT an essential ingredient of a mathematical game?Easy
A) Rules
B) Outcomes and Payoffs
C) Uncertainty of the Outcome
D) Cheating
Explanation:

Correct Answer: D

Game theory does not model cheating — strategies are within the allowed moves. All players follow the rules in mathematical game models.

4. In game theory, what is the key difference between a "move" and a "strategy"?Medium
A) A move is a complete plan, while a strategy is a single action
B) A strategy is a complete plan covering all possible positions, while a move is a single action
C) They are essentially the same concept
D) A move applies to simultaneous games, while a strategy applies to sequential games
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

A strategy is a complete plan specifying what move to make in every possible position in the game, while a move is the choice a player makes in a given position.

5. Which game classification describes a situation where one player's gain equals another's loss?Easy
A) Cooperative game
B) Zero-sum game
C) Sequential game
D) Perfect information game
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

Zero-sum games are those where one player's gain equals another's loss (e.g., Poker, Chess). Real-world games are rarely pure zero-sum.

6. Why is (C, C) not a Nash Equilibrium in the Prisoner's Dilemma?Medium
A) Both players get equal delays
B) Both players have an incentive to switch to D
C) The network becomes unstable
D) It requires communication between players
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

(C, C) is not a Nash Equilibrium because each player has an incentive to unilaterally deviate to strategy D. If one player switches to D while the other remains at C, the switching player improves their outcome (from 1ms to 0ms delay).

7. What is the key assumption about players in game theory?Easy
A) Players are always cooperative
B) Players are irrational
C) Players are rational and try to maximize their own payoff
D) Players don't care about outcomes
Explanation:

Correct Answer: C

The rational behavior assumption states that players are rational: they have preferences, beliefs about other players, and try to maximize their own payoff. They also know that other players are rational and trying to maximize their payoff.

8. In which type of game does a player know all prior moves when making a decision?Medium
A) Simultaneous game
B) Perfect information game
C) Zero-sum game
D) Cooperative game
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

In perfect information games (typically sequential games), a player knows all prior moves when deciding. Chess is a classic example of a perfect information game.

9. What is the fundamental reason why the Prisoner's Dilemma presents a social dilemma?Medium
A) Players cannot communicate with each other
B) The Nash Equilibrium is Pareto inefficient
C) Players have dominant strategies
D) The game is zero-sum
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

The social dilemma in the Prisoner's Dilemma arises because individual rationality leads to an outcome (Nash Equilibrium) that is worse for all players compared to another feasible outcome (mutual cooperation). This tension between individual and collective rationality is the essence of the dilemma.

10. What distinguishes a mixed strategy from a pure strategy in game theory?Medium
A) A pure strategy is deterministic, while a mixed strategy involves randomization
B) A mixed strategy applies only to simultaneous games
C) A pure strategy is always dominant
D) Mixed strategies are only relevant in zero-sum games
Explanation:

Correct Answer: A

A pure strategy is a deterministic plan that specifies one particular action in each situation. A mixed strategy is a probability distribution over pure strategies, introducing intentional randomization.

11. In the TCP Congestion Control game, what would happen to the Nash Equilibrium if the delay for (D,D) was reduced from 3ms to 1.5ms?Hard
A) (C,C) would become the Nash Equilibrium
B) (D,D) would remain the Nash Equilibrium but would be Pareto efficient
C) (D,D) would remain the Nash Equilibrium but the social dilemma would be reduced
D) Both (C,C) and (D,D) would be Nash Equilibria
Explanation:

Correct Answer: C

Reducing the delay for (D,D) from 3ms to 1.5ms would still preserve D as the dominant strategy (since 0ms < 1ms and 1.5ms < 4ms), so (D,D) remains the Nash Equilibrium. However, the difference between the cooperative outcome (1ms) and the equilibrium outcome (1.5ms) is smaller, reducing the severity of the social dilemma.

12. Which of the following conditions would transform the Prisoner's Dilemma into a different game where cooperation becomes sustainable?Hard
A) Making the game sequential rather than simultaneous
B) Introducing repeated interactions with a sufficiently high discount factor
C) Adding communication between players before the game
D) All of the above
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

Only repeated interactions with a sufficiently high discount factor can fundamentally change the strategic structure to make cooperation sustainable in equilibrium. In repeated games, players can use trigger strategies to punish defection, making cooperation rational.

13. What is the key difference between complete information and perfect information in game theory?Medium
A) Complete information refers to knowing all players' payoffs, while perfect information refers to knowing all previous moves
B) They are identical concepts
C) Complete information applies to sequential games, perfect information to simultaneous games
D) Perfect information requires common knowledge of rationality
Explanation:

Correct Answer: A

Complete information means all players know the game structure, including all possible moves and all players' payoff functions. Perfect information means that at each point in the game, players know the entire history of moves made so far.

14. Which of the following is NOT a necessary condition for a Nash Equilibrium?Hard
A) No player can benefit by unilaterally changing their strategy
B) Players have common knowledge of rationality
C) The outcome is Pareto efficient
D) Players are rational utility maximizers
Explanation:

Correct Answer: C

Pareto efficiency is NOT a necessary condition for Nash Equilibrium. The Prisoner's Dilemma clearly demonstrates this - (D,D) is a Nash Equilibrium but is Pareto inferior to (C,C). Nash Equilibrium only requires that no player can unilaterally improve their payoff.

15. In the context of the TCP Congestion Control game, what mechanism could a network operator implement to make (C,C) a Nash Equilibrium?Hard
A) Implement a protocol that detects defective TCP and imposes additional 3ms delay on defectors
B) Increase the bandwidth of the network
C) Allow users to communicate before making decisions
D) Randomly assign TCP implementations to users
Explanation:

Correct Answer: A

To make (C,C) a Nash Equilibrium, the network operator must change the payoff structure so that defection is no longer beneficial. If defectors receive an additional 3ms penalty, then the payoff for defecting when the other cooperates would become worse than mutual cooperation.

16. What is the fundamental difference between cooperative and non-cooperative game theory?Medium
A) Cooperative game theory assumes players can make binding agreements, while non-cooperative game theory does not
B) Cooperative game theory studies zero-sum games, while non-cooperative studies non-zero-sum games
C) Non-cooperative game theory assumes perfect information
D) Cooperative game theory only applies to economic markets
Explanation:

Correct Answer: A

The key distinction is that cooperative game theory allows for binding agreements that players cannot unilaterally deviate from, while non-cooperative game theory assumes all agreements are non-binding ("cheap talk").

17. In a Bayesian game, what represents a player's private information?Hard
A) Their type
B) Their strategy
C) Their payoff function
D) Both A and C
Explanation:

Correct Answer: D

In Bayesian games (games of incomplete information), a player's "type" encapsulates their private information, which can include their payoff function, their capabilities, or their preferences.

18. Which solution concept requires that players' strategies form a Nash Equilibrium in every subgame of the original game?Hard
A) Dominant strategy equilibrium
B) Subgame perfect equilibrium
C) Bayesian Nash equilibrium
D) Correlated equilibrium
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

Subgame perfect equilibrium is a refinement of Nash Equilibrium that applies to extensive-form games (sequential games). It requires that players' strategies constitute a Nash Equilibrium not only in the whole game but in every subgame.

19. What is the key assumption of "common knowledge of rationality" in game theory?Medium
A) All players are rational
B) All players are rational, and all players know that all players are rational
C) All players are rational, all players know that all players are rational, all players know that all players know that all players are rational, and so on ad infinitum
D) Rationality is objectively defined and measurable
Explanation:

Correct Answer: C

Common knowledge of rationality requires an infinite hierarchy of beliefs: (1) all players are rational, (2) all players know (1), (3) all players know (2), (4) all players know (3), and so on.

20. In the TCP Congestion Control game, what would happen if the payoff for (D,C) was changed to (2ms, 2ms)?Hard
A) The game would become a coordination game
B) D would no longer be a dominant strategy
C) Both (C,C) and (D,D) would be Nash Equilibria
D) The game would have no pure strategy Nash Equilibrium
Explanation:

Correct Answer: B

If the payoff for (D,C) becomes (2ms, 2ms), then when the other player chooses C, your payoff for choosing D (2ms) is worse than choosing C (1ms). This means D is no longer always the best response - it's only better when the other player chooses D.
